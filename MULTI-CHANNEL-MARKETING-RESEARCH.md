## 1. MULTI-CHANNEL APPROACH FOR AD GRANTS

### 1.1 How Successful Nonprofits Combine Google Ads with Other Channels

The most effective nonprofits treat Google Ad Grants as ONE piece of a larger marketing ecosystem, not as their entire strategy. Here is how the integration works in practice:

**The Core Principle:** Google Ad Grants captures EXISTING demand (people already searching for something). Other channels CREATE demand (making people aware of you before they search). You need both.

**Typical Channel Mix for a Successful Nonprofit:**

| Channel | Role in Mix | Budget |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| Google Ad Grants | Capture search intent, drive traffic | $10,000/mo (free) |
| Email Marketing | Nurture leads, retain supporters | $50-200/mo (Mailchimp/similar) |
| Social Media (organic) | Build awareness, community | Time only |
| Content Marketing (blog/SEO) | Long-term organic traffic | Time only |
| Social Media (paid) | Targeted awareness, remarketing | $200-1000/mo if budget allows |
| Google Ads (paid account) | Remarketing, Display, YouTube | $200-500/mo if budget allows |

**Key Insight from Specialists:** The average Ad Grant account only uses $300 out of the $10,000 monthly budget. However, accounts managed by specialists averaged $8,650 in monthly spend in January 2026, with 68% exceeding $9,000. The difference is strategic integration with other channels that drive branded search volume.

**How Multi-Channel Feeds Google Ads:**
- Social media posts about a topic -> people Google that topic -> your Ad Grant captures them
- Email newsletter mentions a resource -> some recipients Google it later -> Ad Grant captures them
- Blog post ranks organically AND you run ads on same topic -> you dominate the SERP
- Conference presentation -> attendees Google your journal -> Ad Grant captures them

### 1.2 Typical Marketing Funnel for a Nonprofit

The nonprofit marketing funnel has four stages, each served by different channels:

**Stage 1: AWARENESS (Top of Funnel)**
- Goal: Make potential authors/readers aware AIR exists
- Channels: Google Ad Grants (broad keywords), social media, content marketing, ResearchGate, conference presence
- Messaging: "Discover a new peer-reviewed journal focused on [topics]"
- Metric: Impressions, website visits, social reach
- For nonprofits specifically: "Your job is to spark interest, not ask for submissions. Introduce your mission, highlight your values, and build trust."

**Stage 2: CONSIDERATION (Middle of Funnel)**
- Goal: Get potential authors to seriously consider submitting to AIR
- Channels: Blog posts, landing pages, email marketing, Google Ads (specific keywords)
- Messaging: "Why publish with AIR? Fast peer review, open access, impact-focused"
- Metric: Time on site, pages per session, email signups, return visits

**Stage 3: CONVERSION (Bottom of Funnel)**
- Goal: Get authors to actually submit manuscripts; get readers to cite articles
- Channels: Direct email, Google Ads (high-intent keywords), retargeting (paid account)
- Messaging: "Submit your manuscript today" with clear CTAs
- Metric: Manuscript submissions, account registrations, newsletter signups

**Stage 4: RETENTION/LOYALTY**
- Goal: Turn one-time authors into repeat contributors; build reviewer pool
- Channels: Email newsletters, social media community, publication certificates
- Messaging: "Join our growing community of researchers"
- Metric: Repeat submissions, reviewer signups, referrals

### 1.3 How to Use Organic Search + Paid Search Together

**Strategic Keyword Division:**

Do NOT target the exact same keywords for both SEO and Google Ads. Instead:

- **SEO (organic):** Focus on long-tail, informational keywords that are less competitive
  - Example: "how to publish interdisciplinary research paper"
  - Example: "open access journals accepting undergraduate research"
  - Example: "peer review process for new authors explained"

- **Google Ads (paid/grant):** Prioritize transactional and high-intent keywords
  - Example: "submit research paper online"
  - Example: "peer reviewed journal call for papers"
  - Example: "open access journal publication"

**Measuring the Synergy:**
Use Google Analytics to track how many times organic search was a touchpoint in a journey that ultimately converted through a paid ad. This proves the value of top-of-funnel SEO content. Calculate a blended cost-per-acquisition across both channels -- as SEO gains traction and brings in more "free" conversions, it lowers your overall blended CPA.

**Practical Application for AIR:**
1. Write blog posts about research methodology, publishing tips, academic writing
2. These rank organically for informational queries
3. Readers discover AIR through blog content
4. Later, they search for "submit paper to journal" -> your Ad Grant ad appears
5. They recognize your brand -> higher CTR -> conversion

### 1.6 How to Build Remarketing Audiences from Multiple Channels

**CRITICAL LIMITATION:** Google Ad Grants accounts do NOT have access to remarketing, the Google Display Network, or image/video ads. Remarketing requires a separate PAID Google Ads account.

3. **Cross-Channel Audience Building Strategy:**
   - Week 1-4: Run Ad Grant campaigns to drive traffic, install Google Analytics tag
   - Week 1-4: Simultaneously grow email list through content offers
   - Week 5+: Once you have 100+ website visitors in an audience, you CAN use a paid account for remarketing
   - Ongoing: Upload email lists monthly to Customer Match

**Practical Audience Segments for AIR:**

- "Blog readers interested in publishing tips" (nurture audience)
- "Email subscribers who opened 3+ emails" (engaged audience)

## 2. GOOGLE ADS + SOCIAL MEDIA INTEGRATION

### 2.1 How Social Media Presence Affects Google Ads Performance

Social media does NOT directly affect Google Ads Quality Score. Quality Score is based on three factors only:
1. Expected CTR
2. Ad relevance
3. Landing page experience

However, social media INDIRECTLY supports Google Ads performance through:

**Brand Search Volume Effect:**
- Active social media presence -> more people know your brand -> more people search for your brand name -> branded searches have very high CTR -> this boosts your account-level CTR -> helps maintain the 5% CTR requirement
- Google Trends data shows branded search volume over time. Strong social presence correlates with higher branded search volume.

**Content Amplification:**
- Share blog content on social media -> some readers later search for related topics -> your ads appear -> they recognize your brand -> higher CTR on your ads

**Trust Signals:**
- When someone sees your ad, they may quickly Google your organization
- Finding active social profiles, reviews, and community engagement builds trust
- This increases the likelihood of clicking your ad AND converting

### 2.2 Can You Use LinkedIn Audiences in Google Ads?

**Short answer: Not directly.** LinkedIn and Google Ads are separate platforms with separate audience data.

**However, there are workarounds:**

1. **LinkedIn Lead Gen -> Google Customer Match:**
   - Run LinkedIn campaigns or collect leads via LinkedIn
   - Export email addresses
   - Upload to Google Ads as Customer Match audience
   - Now you can target those LinkedIn users on Google

2. **LinkedIn Insight Tag -> Website Audience:**
   - Install LinkedIn Insight Tag on your website
   - Use LinkedIn's website audience feature to analyze who visits your site
   - Use that demographic/firmographic data to inform your Google Ads targeting

3. **Google Ads Demographic Targeting:**
   - While not as precise as LinkedIn, Google Ads offers demographic targeting (age, gender, income, education level)
   - Use Google's "Detailed Demographics" to target people by education level (useful for academic journals)

4. **UTM-Based Segmentation:**
   - Tag all LinkedIn traffic with UTMs
   - In Google Analytics, analyze LinkedIn visitors' behavior
   - Create Google Ads audiences based on behavior patterns

### 2.4 How to Track Attribution Across Channels

**Google Analytics 4 (GA4) is Your Hub:**
- All channels feed into GA4
- Use the "Attribution" reports to see which channels contribute to conversions
- Default attribution model in GA4 is data-driven (uses machine learning)

**Multi-Touch Attribution Models:**

| Model | How It Works | Best For |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Last Click | 100% credit to final touchpoint | Understanding what closes deals |
| First Click | 100% credit to first touchpoint | Understanding what creates awareness |
| Linear | Equal credit to all touchpoints | Simple fairness across channels |
| Data-Driven | ML assigns credit based on patterns | Most accurate (requires data volume) |

**Practical Attribution Setup for AIR:**
1. Install GA4 on your website
2. Set up conversion events (manuscript submission, newsletter signup, account creation)
3. Use UTM parameters on ALL external links
4. Review "Conversion Paths" report monthly to see multi-touch journeys
5. Implement both first-touch and last-touch tracking

### 2.5 UTM Parameters Best Practices

**The Five Standard UTM Parameters:**

| Parameter | Purpose | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| utm_source | Where traffic comes from | google, linkedin, newsletter |
| utm_medium | Type of channel | cpc, social, email |
| utm_campaign | Specific promotion name | spring2026_cfp, author_guide |
| utm_term | Keyword (paid search) | submit_research_paper |
| utm_content | Differentiates ad variations | headline_a, headline_b |

**Critical Rules:**

1. **Always use lowercase** -- "Facebook" and "facebook" and "FACEBOOK" are three separate sources in analytics. Use lowercase only.
2. **No spaces** -- Use underscores or hyphens: `spring_2026_cfp` not `spring 2026 cfp`
3. **Be consistent** -- Create a naming convention document and follow it religiously
4. **Don't use .com in source names** -- Use `linkedin` not `linkedin.com`
5. **If it's a paid ad, the source is the platform you're paying** -- If retargeting on Facebook through AdRoll, source is `adroll` not `facebook`

**UTM Template for AIR:**

```
Website links from social media:
?utm_source=linkedin&utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=cfp_spring2026&utm_content=post_research_impact

Email newsletter links:
?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=march2026_digest&utm_content=cta_submit

Google Ad Grant ads:
(Auto-tagged by Google -- do NOT manually add UTMs to Google Ads)

Conference handout QR code:
?utm_source=conference&utm_medium=qr_code&utm_campaign=asa2026_booth

ResearchGate profile link:
?utm_source=researchgate&utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=profile_link
```

**CRM Integration:**
Pass UTM parameters through your lead capture forms into your CRM as custom fields. Use first-touch attribution fields that only populate once and never overwrite, so you always know the original source of each contact.

**Regular Audits:**
Open an incognito window monthly, click a tracked URL, complete a conversion, and verify the UTM parameters recorded correctly in your analytics.

## 3. MARKET SEGMENTATION FOR FIRST CAMPAIGNS

### 3.1 How Professional Marketers Choose Which Markets to Target First

**The Priority Matrix Approach:**

Professional marketers evaluate potential market segments on three axes:
1. **Size** -- How many potential authors/readers are in this segment?
2. **Intent** -- How likely are they to actually submit/engage?
3. **Competition** -- How many other journals compete for their attention?

**For AIR Specifically, Evaluate These Segments:**

| Segment | Size | Intent | Competition | Priority |
|---------|------|--------|-------------|----------|
| US graduate students seeking first publication | Large | High | Medium | HIGH |
| International researchers wanting US journal credit | Very Large | High | High | MEDIUM |
| Undergraduate honors thesis authors | Medium | Medium | Low | HIGH |
| Established faculty seeking new venues | Large | Low | Very High | LOW |
| Independent researchers / practitioners | Medium | Medium | Low | MEDIUM |
| Policy professionals with research | Small | Medium | Low | MEDIUM |

**The "Beachhead Market" Strategy:**
Rather than trying to reach everyone, identify ONE segment you can dominate first. Win that segment, build case studies and testimonials, then expand.

Recommended beachhead for AIR: **US graduate students and early-career researchers** seeking their first or second publication in a peer-reviewed journal. They have high intent, they actively search for opportunities, and elite journals don't serve them well.

### 3.2 Should You Start Narrow or Broad?

**Start NARROW, then expand.** Here is why:

**Arguments for Starting Narrow:**
- With Ad Grants' $10,000/month, spreading too thin means no single segment gets enough impressions
- Narrow targeting = more relevant ads = higher CTR = easier to maintain 5% CTR requirement
- Easier to write compelling ad copy when you know exactly who you're talking to
- Faster feedback loop -- you learn what works quickly
- Better landing page relevance (you can tailor pages to specific audience)

**Arguments for Starting Broad (and why they're weaker for you):**
- More data for Google's algorithms (but you need consistent data, not scattered data)
- Discover unexpected audiences (but you can expand later once fundamentals work)

**Recommended Approach:**
1. **Month 1-2:** 2-3 tightly targeted campaigns for your beachhead market
2. **Month 3-4:** Add 1-2 more campaigns for adjacent segments based on learnings
3. **Month 5-6:** Expand to broader audiences and international markets
4. **Ongoing:** 70% budget on proven segments, 20% on tests, 10% on experiments

### 3.3 Geographic Testing Strategy

**Phase 1 (Month 1-3): US Only**
- Start with United States targeting
- Reason: Ad Grants are most competitive domestically, your nonprofit is US-based, English-language searchers
- Within US, no sub-geographic targeting needed initially -- let Google optimize

**Phase 2 (Month 3-6): English-Speaking Countries**
- Add: UK, Canada, Australia
- Create SEPARATE campaigns for each country (geotargeting is campaign-level only)
- This lets you compare performance and budget by country
- Run lower budgets in new geos to test the waters

**Phase 3 (Month 6+): International Expansion**
- Add: India, Nigeria, Philippines, other countries with large English-speaking academic populations
- Consider: Where do most of your website visitors already come from? (Check GA4)
- Consider: Which countries have researchers actively seeking international publication?

**Important Setting:** In Google Ads location targeting, use "Presence" (people IN the location) not "Presence or Interest" (people interested in the location). For an academic journal, you want people physically in the target country.

### 3.4 Language Targeting Considerations

**For AIR (English-language journal):**
- Target English language for all campaigns
- Do NOT target non-English languages even for international campaigns
- Reason: Your journal publishes in English, your website is in English, your submission process is in English
- Researchers searching in English are self-selecting as capable of writing in English

**Exception to Consider Later:**
- Some researchers in non-English countries search in their native language for "publish in American journal" (in their language)
- This is an advanced strategy for later -- don't start here

### 3.5 Budget Allocation Across Test Campaigns

**The 70/20/10 Model:**
- 70% of daily budget ($230/day) -> Proven, high-performing campaigns
- 20% of daily budget ($66/day) -> Testing new keywords, audiences, ad copy
- 10% of daily budget ($33/day) -> Experimental campaigns (new geos, topics, angles)

**Initial Campaign Budget Split (Month 1-2):**

Since everything is new, start with equal budgets and let data guide reallocation:

| Campaign | Daily Budget | Focus |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Campaign 1: Call for Papers / Submit | $100/day | High-intent researchers |
| Campaign 2: Open Access Journal | $100/day | Researchers seeking OA venues |
| Campaign 3: Academic Publishing Resources | $80/day | Content/awareness play |
| Reserve for testing | $49/day | New ideas |

**After 30 Days:** Reallocate based on performance. Double down on what works. Pause what doesn't.

**Reality Check:** Most small nonprofits struggle to spend even half their allocation initially. A new account might realistically use $2,000-4,000/month while learning. That is normal and acceptable -- focus on quality over quantity.

### 3.6 How to Identify Your Best-Performing Audience Segment

**Data-Driven Segment Identification Process:**

1. **Week 1-2: Cast a Moderate Net**
   - Run 3-4 campaigns targeting different keyword themes
   - Don't optimize yet -- gather data

2. **Week 3-4: Analyze First Signals**
   - Which campaigns have highest CTR? (relevance signal)
   - Which keywords drive the most conversions?
   - Which demographics engage most? (Check GA4 demographics report)
   - Which geographic areas perform best?

3. **Month 2: Segment Deep-Dive**
   - In GA4, look at conversion paths by audience
   - Check: Desktop vs. mobile (academics often search from desktop at work)
   - Check: Time of day / day of week patterns
   - Check: Which landing pages convert best

4. **Month 3: Refine and Double Down**
   - Create dedicated campaigns for your best segment
   - Write ad copy specifically for that audience
   - Build landing pages tailored to their needs
   - Allocate 70% of budget to proven winners

**Key Metrics to Compare Segments:**
- CTR (must be above 5% for compliance)
- Conversion Rate
- Cost per Conversion (even though it's free money, efficiency matters)
- Time on Site (are they actually reading?)
- Pages per Session
- Return Visit Rate

## 4. CAMPAIGN TESTING FRAMEWORK

### 4.1 Alpha-Beta Campaign Structure

This is the gold standard PPC testing framework, now evolved for 2025-2026:

**BETA Campaigns (Prospecting/Discovery):**
- Purpose: Find new high-performing keywords
- Match Types: Broad match and phrase match keywords
- Budget: Smaller (20-30% of total)
- Bids: Lower bids, automated bidding (Maximize Clicks)
- Structure: Themed ad groups with 5-10 related keywords each
- Goal: Discover which search queries actually convert

**ALPHA Campaigns (Proven Performers):**
- Purpose: Maximize results from known winners
- Match Types: Exact match keywords only
- Budget: Larger (70-80% of total)
- Bids: Higher bids, Maximize Conversions bidding
- Structure: Single Theme Ad Groups (STAGs) with 2-3 closely related keywords
- Goal: Dominate the SERPs for your best keywords

**GAMMA Campaigns (Experimental):**
- Purpose: Test completely new ideas, audiences, or campaign types
- Budget: Small (5-10%)
- Examples: Performance Max (now available in Ad Grants for Search/Maps), new topic areas, seasonal campaigns

**The Migration Process:**

```
BETA Campaign (broad/phrase match)
  -> Search Terms Report reveals high-performing queries
  -> Add those queries as exact match in ALPHA Campaign
  -> Add those queries as NEGATIVE keywords in BETA (prevent overlap)
  -> ALPHA gets the budget, BETA keeps prospecting

GAMMA Campaign (experimental)
  -> If something works, promote to BETA or ALPHA
  -> If it doesn't work after 30 days, pause it
```

**Evolution Note (2025-2026):**
The strict SKAG (Single Keyword Ad Group) approach has evolved. Google's expanded close variants and smart bidding work best with themed keyword groupings (STAGs). Instead of one keyword per ad group, aim for 2-3 closely related keywords per ad group in your Alpha campaigns.

### 4.4 How to Write Ad Copy That Maintains 5% CTR

**The 5% CTR Requirement Explained:**
- Measured at the ACCOUNT level, not per keyword or ad
- If you fall below 5% for 2 consecutive months, your account can be deactivated
- Well-optimized accounts typically achieve 10%+ CTR
- Branded keywords (your journal name) will have very high CTR (20-40%), which helps the average

**Ad Copy Tactics for High CTR:**

1. **Use Dynamic Keyword Insertion (DKI):**
   - Headline: `{KeyWord:Submit Your Research}`
   - This inserts the user's actual search term into your headline
   - Massively increases relevance and CTR

2. **Include Numbers and Specifics:**
   - "Peer Review in 4-6 Weeks" beats "Fast Peer Review"
   - "Published Since 2025" beats "Established Journal"
   - "500+ Authors Published" beats "Many Authors Trust Us"

3. **Strong Calls to Action:**
   - "Submit Your Manuscript Today"
   - "Get Published -- Apply Now"
   - "Free Open Access -- Submit Now"

4. **Address Pain Points:**
   - "No Publication Fees -- Submit Free"
   - "Quick Review -- No Long Waits"
   - "First-Time Authors Welcome"

5. **Use Sitelink Extensions:**
   - Add 4+ sitelinks: "Submission Guidelines", "About Our Review Process", "Current Issue", "Author Resources"
   - Sitelinks increase CTR by giving searchers more options

6. **Negative Keywords to Protect CTR:**
   - Add negatives for irrelevant terms that get impressions but no clicks
   - Examples: "predatory", "scam", "free download", "pirate", specific other journal names
   - Review Search Terms Report weekly and add negatives aggressively

### 4.5 Responsive Search Ads (RSA) Best Practices

**Use All Available Assets:**
- Provide all 15 headlines and 4 descriptions
- The more inputs you give Google, the more combinations it can test
- Google needs 2,000-3,000 impressions per ad to optimize effectively

**Strategic Pinning:**
- Pin your brand name or primary CTA to Position 1 headline (always shows)
- Pin a key differentiator to Position 2
- Do NOT pin more than 2-3 assets total -- over-pinning reduces Google's ability to optimize
- Pinning just one headline can cut testing potential by 75%

**Create Multiple RSA Variations:**
- Aim for 2-3 RSAs per ad group (Ad Grants requires minimum 2)
- Each RSA should take a different angle:
  - RSA 1: Problem-focused ("Struggling to Get Published?")
  - RSA 2: Solution-focused ("Fast Peer Review, Free Publication")
  - RSA 3: Benefit-focused ("Build Your CV with a US Publication")

**Asset Performance Ratings:**
- Google rates each headline/description as "Best", "Good", or "Low"
- After 30 days, replace "Low" performing assets with new variations
- Keep "Best" performers -- they're working

**Minimum Data Requirements:**

| Metric | Minimum for Significance | Typical Timeline |
|--------|-------------------------|-----------------|
| CTR testing (ad copy A vs B) | 1,000+ impressions per variant | 2-4 weeks |
| Conversion rate testing | 500-1,000 clicks per variant | 4-8 weeks |
| Keyword performance | 200+ impressions per keyword | 1-2 weeks |
| Campaign-level decisions | 30 days minimum | 30 days |
| Geographic testing | 1,000+ impressions per geo | 2-4 weeks |

**Confidence Levels:**
- Google uses 95% confidence interval for experiments
- The more important a keyword is to your account, the higher confidence you want (aim for 95%+)
- Never take action on less than 90% confidence

**Practical Timeline for AIR:**
- **Week 1-2:** Data collection only. Resist the urge to change things.
- **Week 3-4:** First optimizations: pause clearly bad keywords (QS < 3), add negative keywords
- **Month 2:** First ad copy optimizations based on CTR data
- **Month 3:** First major restructuring based on conversion data
- **Ongoing:** Continuous testing, but with minimum 2-week test cycles

### 4.7 A/B Testing Methodology for Nonprofits

**Test One Variable at a Time:**
- Don't change headline AND description AND landing page simultaneously
- Change one thing, measure impact, then change the next

**What to A/B Test (in priority order):**

1. **Landing Pages** (biggest impact on conversion rate)
   - Test: Long-form vs. short-form submission page
   - Test: With/without author testimonials
   - Test: Different value propositions above the fold

2. **Ad Headlines** (biggest impact on CTR)
   - Test: Emotional vs. factual headlines
   - Test: With/without numbers
   - Test: Question vs. statement format

3. **Keywords** (ongoing)
   - Test: Broad match vs. phrase match performance
   - Test: Generic vs. specific terms

4. **Audience Targeting** (campaign level)
   - Test: US only vs. US + international
   - Test: Different demographic settings

**Documentation:**
Keep a testing log:
```
Test #: 001
Hypothesis: "Including 'No Fees' in headline will increase CTR by 2%"
Start Date: March 15, 2026
End Date: March 29, 2026
Variant A: Current headline without "No Fees"
Variant B: Headline with "No Publication Fees"
Metric: CTR
Result: [fill in after test]
Confidence: [fill in]
Action: [implement winner / test further / inconclusive]
```

## 5. COMPETITOR INTELLIGENCE

### 5.1 Tools for Spying on Competitor Ads

**Free Tools:**

1. **Google Ads Transparency Center** (ads.google.com/transparency)
   - Shows every ad currently running by any verified advertiser on Google
   - Completely free
   - Shows ad formats (text, video, image), geographic targeting, and date ranges
   - "Gives you 99% of what you need when it comes to spying on competitor ads"
   - How to use: Search for competitor names or relevant keywords

2. **Google Keyword Planner** (inside Google Ads)
   - Shows search volume and competition for keywords
   - Free with a Google Ads account (Ad Grant counts)
   - Shows estimated bid ranges

3. **Google Search Results** (manual method)
   - Search for your target keywords in incognito mode
   - Note which competitors appear as ads
   - Screenshot their ad copy for reference

**Paid Tools (consider later):**

4. **SpyFu** (spyfu.com)
   - Shows every keyword a competitor has bid on
   - Every ad variation they've tested
   - Estimates of how much they've spent
   - Historical ad data going back years
   - Free tier available with limited data
   - Paid: ~$39/month

5. **SEMrush** (semrush.com)
   - Comprehensive competitor analysis
   - Ad copy research, keyword gaps
   - Paid: ~$129/month (overkill for starting out)

6. **SimilarWeb** (similarweb.com)
   - Shows traffic sources for any website
   - Free tier shows basic data
   - Good for understanding competitors' channel mix

### 5.3 Nonprofit Journals Using Ad Grants Successfully

There is limited public data specifically about academic journals using Ad Grants. However, relevant case studies from similar nonprofits show:

**Comparable Success Stories:**
- **Glaucoma Research Foundation:** Over 600 people spend 5+ minutes on their website each month after finding it through an Ad Grant
- **Jeannette Rankin Foundation:** 92.7% increase in clicks quarter-over-quarter with 4,300 additional clicks
- **Habitat for Humanity (East Bay/Silicon Valley):** Web conversions grew from 300 to 500+ per month (17% increase)

**Why Academic Journal Ad Grant Data Is Scarce:**
- Most academic publishers are commercial entities (Elsevier, Springer) and don't qualify for Ad Grants
- Nonprofit academic societies (APA, AMA, etc.) have Ad Grants but don't publicize campaign details
- University press operations may use institutional Ad Grant accounts

**What This Means for AIR:**
You're in a relatively uncompeted space for Ad Grants. Most academic journal advertising is done with PAID Google Ads accounts by commercial publishers. As a 501(c)(3), you have a cost advantage -- $10,000/month free vs. their paid campaigns.

### 5.4 Practical Competitor Research Workflow

**Step-by-Step Process:**

1. **Identify Your Real Competitors** (not Elsevier -- they're not competing for the same authors)
   - Other open access journals with no APCs
   - Other new/emerging journals
   - Other interdisciplinary journals
   - Other journals targeting early-career researchers

2. **Search Transparency Center:**
   - Search each competitor name
   - Screenshot all active ads
   - Note their messaging angles and landing pages

3. **Keyword Gap Analysis:**
   - Use Google Keyword Planner

   - Find keywords they rank for that you don't

4. **Landing Page Analysis:**
   - Visit competitor landing pages from their ads
   - Note: Layout, CTAs, value propositions, social proof
   - Identify what you can do better

5. **Document Everything:**
   - Create a competitive intelligence spreadsheet
   - Update monthly
   - Track changes in competitor messaging over time

- Must have a clear peer review process described on website
- Must display an open access statement
- Each article must be available as an individual full-text article at its own URL
- After inclusion: website traffic typically increases THREEFOLD

### 6.5 Indexing as Marketing

**Google Scholar Indexing:**
- 389+ million records -- the most comprehensive scholarly index
- Google Scholar does NOT accept direct manual submissions
- To get indexed: proper HTML meta tags, machine-readable metadata, individual article URLs
- Timeline for new journals: 6-9 months to establish trust with Scholar's algorithms

**Best Practices for Google Scholar Indexing:**
1. Join Crossref and register references (reference linking) so citations are machine-readable
2. Use proper meta tags (highwire press tags, Dublin Core, or schema.org)
3. Make full-text PDFs accessible with clear title and author metadata
4. Publish an explicit "For indexers" page describing where metadata and full texts are located
5. Ensure each article has its own stable URL

**Crossref as Marketing:**
- Assigns DOIs to your articles (standard academic identifier)
- Makes your articles discoverable across thousands of platforms
- Enables citation linking -- when other papers cite yours, the link works
- Cost: ~$275/year membership + $1 per DOI registered
- Essential for credibility and discoverability

**ORCID Integration:**
- Encourage authors to link publications to their ORCID profiles
- This creates backlinks and increases visibility
- Many institutions require ORCID IDs
